Understanding Circular Saw Specifications

Blade Diameter (mm)

Blade diameter refers to the overall size of the blade the tool is designed to use. Larger diameters allow deeper cuts and greater cutting capacity, making them better for thick stock and heavy-duty work. Smaller diameters enable more compact, lightweight tool designs that are easier to control and better suited for precision work or confined spaces. Choose the blade size that balances cutting needs with portability and handling. Always use the manufacturer-specified blade diameter to ensure the blade clears guards and housings, maintains proper speed and torque, and meets safety and performance requirements.

Blade Bore (mm)

The blade bore is the diameter of the central hole in the blade that fits onto the toolâ€™s spindle. It must match the spindle size or be properly adapted to ensure a secure fit.

Using the correct bore (or the proper reducer/adapter supplied by the manufacturer) is essential to maintain blade stability, minimize vibration, and prevent slippage during cutting. An incorrect bore can cause runout, excessive wear, or unsafe operation.

Max Cutting Depth at 90Â° (mm)

This specification indicates the deepest cut the tool can make when the blade is perpendicular to the material (a straight 90Â° cut). It represents the maximum single-pass cutting capability and is crucial for tasks requiring full-thickness cuts. When selecting a saw for through-cuts, trimming, or plunge operations, ensure the 90Â° depth meets the material thickness you need to cut without multiple passes or special setups.

Max Cutting Depth at 45Â° (mm)

Max cutting depth at 45Â° is the deepest achievable cut with the blade tilted to 45 degrees. Tilting the blade reduces its effective depth, so this value is typically less than the 90Â° depth. This spec is important for bevel cuts, miters, and angled work where consistent depth and clean bevels are required. Plan cuts and choose blade size accordingly if frequent angled cuts are part of your workflow.

RPM (Revolutions Per Minute)

RPM indicates the rotational speed of the drill chuck. Variable speed control allows lower speeds for accurate, controlled drilling and higher speeds for fast, efficient drilling in softer materials such as timber.
